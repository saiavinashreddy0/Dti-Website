Chapter 1 · Problem Statement

Why Students Stay
Silent in Class

Classrooms are meant for interaction — but psychological barriers stop most students from ever speaking up.

😰

Fear of Judgement

Students fear sounding "dumb" in front of peers. Social pressure prevents open expression of doubts, even when understanding is critically needed.

🤐

Silence Misread as Understanding

Teachers interpret classroom silence as comprehension. In reality, silence hides deep confusion and unresolved learning gaps that compound over time.

🧠

Psychological Barriers

Social anxiety, peer comparison, and fear of negative evaluation are deeply embedded emotional barriers — not technical ones.

📉

Compounding Learning Gaps

Unaddressed doubts accumulate, leading to poor performance, reduced confidence, and long-term academic setbacks.

"When a teacher asks 'Does anyone have doubts?', the classroom often remains silent — not because students understand, but because they're afraid to admit they don't."

— Classroom Observation, Empathy Phase 2025–26

Chapters 3–9 · Design Thinking Methodology

Six Phases of Human-Centred
Problem Solving

Click each phase to explore what was discovered, built, and learned.

Phase 01 · Empathize

Understanding Users at the Deepest Level

The empathy phase is the foundation of Design Thinking. Instead of assuming what the problem is, the team collected real-world data by directly interacting with users — students and teachers. Multiple tools were used to uncover hidden emotional and behavioural patterns.

Student surveys distributed — 72% hesitate to ask doubts
Teacher interviews revealed silence misread as understanding
Classroom observations: only confident students dominate
Empathy mapping: fear, anxiety, social pressure identified
Phase 02 · Define

Crystallising the Real Problem

Raw empathy data was synthesised into a precise, actionable problem statement. This phase transformed observations into clearly defined user needs, POV statements, and "How Might We?" questions that guided ideation.

Root cause: lack of psychological safety, not technical failure
POV statements created for 3 key personas
6 "How Might We?" questions generated for ideation
Functional, non-functional & UX requirements defined
Phase 03 · Ideate

Generating & Filtering 30+ Ideas

Individual brainwriting, SCAMPER technique, and mind mapping were used to generate over 30 ideas. Divergent thinking first, then convergent — evaluated against feasibility, simplicity, adoption, and cost.

30+ ideas generated via SCAMPER + brainwriting
Idea matrix scored: QR System 9/10, App 6/10, LMS 5/10
SCAMPER applied: Substitute verbal → digital anonymous
Final selection: QR + anonymous form + teacher dashboard
Phase 04 · Prototype

From Paper Sketches to Functional System

Three levels of prototyping: paper-based (low-fidelity), Wizard of Oz (simulated), and functional (Google Forms + QR). Each level informed the next. Users tested and gave real feedback at every stage.

Paper prototype: students simulated doubt submission
Wizard of Oz: 300% more questions than verbal method
Functional: Google Forms + QR + Sheets dashboard
Key learning: simplicity drives adoption
Phase 05 · Test

Validating in Real Classroom Scenarios

Three test scenarios: during lecture, after lecture, and teacher analytics view. Usability testing, post-usage surveys, and observation testing were conducted. Results were overwhelmingly positive.

Scenario A (during lecture): 4× increase in questions
Scenario B (after lecture): deeper, more conceptual doubts
Scenario C (teacher): identified patterns, adjusted teaching
Student feedback: "I finally asked what I always wanted to"
Phase 06 · Implement

A 6-Week Deployment Roadmap

A structured implementation plan covers awareness creation, QR placement, teacher training, live usage, review, and full integration. Barriers and mitigation strategies are defined for every scenario.

Week 1: awareness posters + QR code placement
Weeks 2–3: pilot testing + initial usage
Weeks 4–5: feedback collection + improvements
Week 6: full integration into classroom routine
Chapters 5–6 · The Solution

QR-Based Anonymous
Doubt Submission System

Simple enough to use in 30 seconds, powerful enough to transform classroom interaction.

1

Teacher Generates QR Code

A unique QR code linked to the anonymous form is placed in the classroom or projected on screen before each session.

2

Student Scans & Submits

Students scan with their phone, type their doubt, select a category, and hit submit — completely anonymously.

3

Real-Time Dashboard

All submitted doubts appear instantly in the teacher's view, sorted by category, with pending/answered status.

4

Upvoting & Prioritization

Students upvote existing doubts they share — teachers instantly see what's most common and address it first.

5

Response & FAQ Generation

Teachers respond verbally or digitally. Answered doubts are saved as FAQs for future reference and revision.
